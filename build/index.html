ELECTION

Absentee balloting helped drive Minnesota's highest voter turnout in six decades

By Jeff Hargarten
Star Tribune November 10, 2020

Nearly 8-in-10 eligible Minnesotans voted in this year's historic election, reaching rates not seen in 60 years and retaining its status as a top-voting state, made largely possible by enthusiasm among both parties and record-shattering numbers of absentee ballots cast during a pandemic.

The last time Minnesota's voter turnout popped above 79 percent was John F. Kennedy's presidential election win in 1960. Prior to that, Minnesota's highest post-war turnout rate was in 1956, when President Dwight D. Eisenhower won reelection, one of the few times the state has landed in the Republican column and drawn voter involvement beyond 80 percent. The 2004 reelection of George W. Bush had similar – but slightly lower – turnout than this year.

A high-stakes election saw this extradinarily high turnout deliver some very close races downballot, plus a big win for Democratic candidate Former Vice President Joe Biden over President Donald Trump.

More than 101 million early votes are estimated to have been cast nationally this year, according to the United States Elections Project. More than 1.8 million of those came from Minnesotans, representing about 56 percent of the total statewide vote and shattering early voting records.

Absentee balloting transcended regional boundaries, with many Minnesota counties seeing at least half of their votes being cast before Election Day.

Some counties saw greater surges in voter turnout than others compared to 2016, with high levels of voter engagement in heavily DFL areas like Hennepin and Ramsey helping propel decisive Democratic wins in statewide contests.

Yet it was counties like Carver, Washington and Scott in the Twin Cities metro area suburbs that represented the highest turnout rates in Minnesota this year, another signal of significant suburban mobilization in favor of Democrats.

Turnout was up all across the country, as Joe Biden claimed the presidency with more than 76 million votes nationwide, while President Trump garnered more votes than any previous incumbant president with 71 million.

Current estimates show Minnesota narrowly leading state-by-state voter turnout rankings, once again securing a status that has long distinguished Minnesotans as a state boasting high levels of civic engagement.
